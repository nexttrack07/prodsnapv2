# Media Buying: Angles, Hooks, and Creative Strategy

This note explains how to think about angles and hooks when planning Meta ads, using Allbirds shoes as the example.

## Core Definitions

An **angle** is the strategic way you frame the offer.

A **hook** is the first line, visual, or idea that grabs attention and gets someone to stop scrolling.

Simple rule:

- **Angle:** the reason someone should care.
- **Hook:** the opening move that gets them to listen.

For example, for a skincare product:

- Angle: This helps busy moms look less tired without a complicated routine.
- Hooks:
  - Still look exhausted after 8 hours of sleep?
  - The 30-second skincare step moms are using before school drop-off.
  - Your concealer is working overtime because your moisturizer is not.

The angle is the broader persuasion strategy. The hooks are different ways to introduce that strategy in the ad.

## What A Media Buyer Usually Comes Up With First

A good media buyer usually does **not** start with hooks.

The typical flow is:

1. Customer and product research
2. Angle hypotheses
3. Creative concepts
4. Hooks and scripts
5. Test structure

For Allbirds, the first question is not "What clever hook can I write?"

The better first question is:

> Who is this shoe for, and why would they pick this over Nike, Hoka, Adidas, Vans, Veja, On, or another casual sneaker?

You are looking for buyer psychology, not just product features.

## Step 1: Research The Market

When researching Allbirds, a media buyer would look at:

- Customer reviews: why people buy, why they complain, and what words they use.
- Competitors: Nike, Hoka, On, Veja, Rothy's, casual sneakers, walking shoes.
- Existing Allbirds ads and organic content.
- Product pages.
- Reddit, TikTok, YouTube reviews, and review articles.
- Objections:
  - Are they worth the price?
  - Do they last?
  - Are they actually supportive?
  - Are they stylish?
  - Are they only for tech workers?

Allbirds' public positioning often centers on all-day comfort, lightweight feel, natural materials, sustainability, breathability, washable everyday wear, and simple styling.

## Step 2: Turn Research Into Angle Hypotheses

After research, create angle buckets.

For Allbirds, possible angles include:

### All-Day Comfort

Shoes you can wear all day without thinking about your feet.

### Travel Simplicity

One pair of shoes for the airport, walking, errands, and dinner.

### Casual Office

Comfortable enough for commuting, clean enough for casual office outfits.

### Sustainability

A better everyday sneaker made with natural materials.

### No-Fuss Daily Wear

Lightweight, washable sneakers for people who do not want high-maintenance shoes.

### Warm-Weather Breathability

Breathable everyday shoes that do not feel heavy or sweaty.

### Minimalist Style

The simple sneaker that goes with almost everything.

These are not hooks yet. They are strategic reasons someone might care.

## Step 3: Build Creative Ideas From Each Angle

Once you have angles, ask:

> How do I show this angle visually?

### Comfort Angle: Creative Ideas

- A person puts on Allbirds at 8am and is still wearing them comfortably at 8pm.
- Split-screen: stiff office shoes vs Allbirds.
- UGC testimonial: "I bought these for travel and now wear them every day."
- Walking footage: commute, groceries, dog walk, coffee shop.

### Travel Angle: Creative Ideas

- Packing one pair of shoes for a weekend trip.
- Airport security, city walking, and dinner outfit in the same shoes.
- "The only shoes I packed for 3 days in New York."

### Sustainability Angle: Creative Ideas

- Materials breakdown: eucalyptus or tree fiber, sugarcane sole, natural materials.
- "Your everyday sneaker does not have to feel like plastic."
- Side-by-side comparison of natural material story vs generic synthetic sneaker.

## Step 4: Then Write Hooks

Hooks come after you know the angle and creative concept.

### Comfort Angle Hooks

- I did not realize my sneakers were uncomfortable until I wore these.
- The shoe I keep by the door because it works for everything.
- POV: your 10,000-step day does not end with sore feet.

### Travel Angle Hooks

- I packed one pair of shoes for the whole trip.
- Airport shoes that do not look like airport shoes.
- These replaced three pairs in my weekend bag.

### No-Fuss Angle Hooks

- The washable sneaker I stopped babying.
- White shoes you do not have to treat like museum pieces.
- Comfortable, simple, and machine washable.

## How To Structure A Test

A structured ad test groups hooks under angles.

Example:

- Angle: Travel shoe
- Creative: UGC packing video
- Hook A: I only packed one pair of shoes.
- Hook B: The best travel shoe is not a running shoe.
- Hook C: 3 days, 22,000 steps, one pair of sneakers.

This helps you learn what is actually working:

- If the travel angle wins, make more travel creatives.
- If only one hook wins, iterate on the opener.
- If none work, the angle may be weak, the creative may not prove it, or the audience may not care.

## Practical Mental Model

Use this order:

1. Research gives you insights.
2. Insights become angles.
3. Angles become creative concepts.
4. Creative concepts need hooks.
5. Hooks are tested at the top of the ad.

In real life, the process can be messy. A media buyer might browse creatives during research and notice a hook first. That is fine. But when organizing the campaign, group ideas by angle so the test produces useful learning.

For Allbirds, the first deliverable after research would usually be a list of angle hypotheses, not finished ads:

1. All-day comfort for busy everyday life.
2. One-shoe travel simplicity.
3. Sustainable sneaker without sacrificing comfort.
4. Breathable warm-weather walking shoe.
5. Minimalist sneaker for casual office and lifestyle.
6. Washable low-maintenance daily shoe.

Then pick the strongest three to five angles, create two to four creatives per angle, and write multiple hooks for each.

That is the difference between random ad-making and structured media buying.
